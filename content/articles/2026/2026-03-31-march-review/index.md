I wrote two articles this month: One on my thoughts about [digital age verification laws](/articles/2026/age-verification/) and another on [the current state of generative AI](/articles/2026/ai-greed/). 

Age verification laws are an authoritarian response to an issue that was already solved, parental controls have existed for as long as it was technically possible. Rather than bringing age into the discussion at all we should mandate that optional parental controls be included in every operating system with a standardized API that programs can optionally opt into. The admin user (parents, schools, employers, etc.) can set rules for the other accounts and problem solved. Any person, organization, or government advocating for age digital verification laws beyond what already existed in the past is either: 1. Grossly ignorant of tech and should not be making laws regarding it, or 2. is outright trying to control access to information and should be removed any position of power for being a threat to democracy. 

AI is something I have complex feeling about, but it basically boils down to that current AI use cases are built with evil motivations and an overly capitalist centric idea of reality. There are ways to make generative tech helpful for humans without taking away their agency and devaluing human ability, but there is no way the tech will ever realize that reality as long as profit motivates the largest players in the field. I don't think the tech itself is inherently bad, but the current way it is used is inherently evil. My examples may have been a bit hyperbolic in the article, but I still stand by them.

## Reading

### The Wander Society by Keri Smith

Imagine a secret society where Walt Whitman is the chief god, his Leaves of Grass is the holy text, and wandering is the greatest form of worship. Keri Smith creates this secret society in her book as she presents the idea that people need to disconnect from our fast, always connected, and money focused world by walking, exploring, and doing things for their own sake.

Minus the almost fanatical and cult like narrative, it is a great book and I agree with the overall premise. Her work is a fitting response and cure to the alienation we feel under the current system. Given the political and economic landscape we are seeing spread across the world we need to re-learn how to disconnect, introspect, and engage with the world outside a screen. 

She also proposes that we undertake research that feeds our souls. In this she gives examples of recording the things we see, picking up hobbies, being deeply interested in what excites us, and all the things people affiliate with lifelong learning. This is another thing I deeply resonate with. 

Overall I'd say it is worth reading, but it isn't like The Wander Society is an amazing revelation or anything deeper than what most of us should already know. Her fanatical tone makes it sound like it was something deep and spiritual and could be a little off-putting as is the common trope of borrowing the names of famous thinkers and claiming they were a part of this secret society. It is common sense, distilled into a short book about the love for life and the need to get away from the endless growth death cult that runs our lives. 

Maybe the answer to a death cult is a life cult?

### News

Emanuel Maiberg talks about how the Open Knowledge Association (OKA), run by Jonathan Zimmerman (we need to be naming and shaming the people behind bad decisions instead of letting them hide behind corporate entities) [is using LLM/AI tools to push slop translations onto Wikipedia](https://www.404media.co/ai-translations-are-adding-hallucinations-to-wikipedia-articles/). While I like the idea of making translation faster and easier, generative AI is very much known to be a flawed tool for any serious work. If you had a team of fluent translators proofreading the generated output then things would be okay (if a little distasteful), but the motive behind using AI tools is antithetical to how one can use them effectively. The reason to use them is to move fast and moving fast is not compatible with proofreading. AI makes stuff up, changes the meanings of things to match its biases, and does not understand human language in a meaningful way. In any case, Wikipedia took a stand against OKA's slop producers without outright denying AI translations that are proofread. That is great. AI can speed things up without being completely terrible if used mindfully, the problem is that AI tools are designed to discourage mindfulness.

### Videos

Haggis on Toast created [little cardboard game cases](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=G7InIXoqAJQ) that hold SD cards with DRM free copies of PC games and can launch the games on his computer using an NFC reader with Home Assistant. It is a simple project, but one that really sparks joy. I've seen this done for movies and TV by other people, but it never stops being nice. One of the things that was lost with physical media is tactility. Being able to hold the item in your hand and put it in a device that plays it is a beautiful thing that brings a little extra meaning to the experience.

Lost in Tech did some [research and gives some tips on using screws in 3D printed parts](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8F4G-OyXHJs). Many people will use captive bolts or heat set inserts with machine screws to bind two 3D printed parts together and it works when you need to be able to repeatedly take something apart and put it back together. If you are just needing to screw something together more permanently there are screws that will do the job and eat into the plastic without issue if you know what you are doing. Not only can this be cheaper and use less resources, it can be stronger in some scenarios. Give the video a watch if you use 3D printing to make practical items. 

Zack Freedman goes over his [reverse engineering process for cloning parts for 3D printing](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OcMvTfUfNXo). 3D printing is magic in an age of garbage products that are sold by garbage people. In this case, the garbage product was a Rosewill RSV-4412 server case. They don't sell replacement drive sleds and the ones included are very poorly made, but Zack had way too much money and time invested into it to just give up so he made his own sleds. Thanks to a lack of profit motive and the low batch nature of 3D printing, the [sled model is available for free on Printables](https://www.printables.com/model/1632299-hotswap-disk-sled-for-crappy-rosewill-rsv-series-s) and they are cheap to produce while still being higher quality than the commercial product.

Baylie Fortier has been [transforming a room into a fantasy themed creative office space for herself](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lEx1MSw0Gs4).
