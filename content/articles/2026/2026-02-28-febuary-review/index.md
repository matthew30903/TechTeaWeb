A busy month. Mostly focused on work and my RPG Maker game so I ignored the website a little more than I should have. 

I did publish my [Linux tips for new users](https://techtea.io/articles/2026/new-linux-users/) article this month. Mostly written because I have a friend who keeps following the influencers who jump onto using Arch based distros and then I get calls about things not working. Nothing wrong with Arch or its derivatives, I've used an Arch based distro for years with only a few self-inflicted issues and I'm using NixOS Unstable right now (another thing I would never recommend to a new user). Can't recommend it because rolling release distros require you to be intentional and knowledgeable, something most people do not want to spend the time to do just to use their computers. Experienced people and those really wanting to understand their computer will benefit from an Arch install, but most other people should just use Mint or Fedora if they are adventurous till they are more comfortable with Linux and tinkering. 

## RPG Maker Game

Not much to share, but I've been slowly building out some ideas I had for my RPG Maker game. As the game focuses on magic, I've done a lot of research on how magic systems work in fantasy, other games, and real world mysticism practices. One of my goals from the start was to sell the fantasy of being a magic user in a fantasy world. With that in mind I'm leaning towards a combination of high fantasy ritual magic for magic outside of combat and a simpler item catalyst -> effect based system for combat. I'll give more concrete examples when I have more of the systems built out. 

I've also been thinking about how magic effects daily lives of NPCs. In the setting magic is a banned practice, but in real world cultures people still pray to icons of saints, carry talismans made with passages from holly texts, and more subtle "magic". I want to tie into the world building how folk magic, religious pressure, and the idea that some of it actually works together.

Manu writes a [response](https://manuelmoreale.com/thoughts/ad-blockers-didn-t-help-kill-the-open-web) to Christian Heilmann's post about [how ad blockers killed the open web](https://christianheilmann.com/2025/12/17/ad-blockers-helped-kill-the-open-web/). I'm 100% with Manu on this one. Ad blockers may have harmed a few small and poorly run businesses, but the open web is 1. still here, perhaps less prominent than before, and 2. doesn't need to be completely monetized with awful methods to exist. Our current system does put a lot of pressure on people to make money at all costs, but websites can be cheap to run, not everything is worth paying for, and not everything needs to be a business. If anything appeasement of ad companies and the websites that work with them has done more damage to the open web than anything an ad blocker can do. 

Nicole argues that making [niche solutions to personal problems is an amazing power granted to people with 3D printers and with software engineering/programming skills](https://ntietz.com/blog/making-niche-solutions-is-the-point/). I think we all have those small problems that can be solved with a little creativity. I use a lot of cardboard and 3D printing for small things around my apartment to make living here more bearable and I have some scripts that run on my computer to solve some daily annoyances. Are any of these things useful to other people? Probably not, but thanks to the power of being able to make things for myself it doesn't matter. Everyone should feel empowered to create things that solve their niche problems and bring joy to their lives. A good example is Nicole's [ergonomic laptop setup](https://ntietz.com/blog/3d-printing-my-laptop-ergonomic-setup/). Stewart Hicks talks about how [micro-apartments are becoming more popular in the United States](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ejPOLhfBpy8). These are apartments that are designed to be as small and space efficient as regulations will allow. They are gaining in popularity due to them being somewhat affordable and often being built in desirable locations. The unfortunate thing is, there are not a lot of other housing types being built. It is luxury apartments, single family houses, or these very small micro apartments. They work as many people are staying single, but it also means there are not as many options for couples and families on a budget either. I am glad that these are being built and I pray that it leads to cities allowing for other types of housing. We need a diverse variety of housing types if we want healthy neighborhoods where people can live and prosper.
