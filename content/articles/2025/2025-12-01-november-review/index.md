This was a crazy month, in a good way this time. I have a lot of photos to share that still need to be edited, but I went out of state for the first time in years. 

My employer does not have PTO rollover so at the end of the year employees lose any unused Paid Time Off hours. Yes this is anti-worker, but it is a common practice in the US. In any case, due to that I needed to use as much as possible in the short time left this year and ended up taking the first 4 days of November off as well as the 28th. This left me with more free time than average to do a lot of fun stuff.

## Descanso Gardens

My partner and I went to the beautiful [Descanso Gardens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descanso_Gardens) in La Canada Flintridge, CA. It is a 150-acre botanical garden here in LA. One thing people don't often realize is that there are large pockets of green space out here if you know where to look. So many beautiful plants and animals.

I took far too many photos so it will be a while before I upload them, but please hold me accountable and ask me where the photos are if you don't see me upload them soon.

We also ate at the restaurant on site. Very expensive, but everything they serve was made with local ingredients and tasted very good.

That week we also went to: Little Tokyo, our go-to date spot; Sawtelle Japantown, the multi-lane stroad from the train station did not have a sidewalk and it was raining so we will not be doing this again, but we made the most of it; and The Grove, an out door shopping mall that is far above our economic class made to look like a pedestrianized European city street. It was a busy week full of adventure.

## Los Angeles Museum of Love

The [LA Museum of Love](https://www.losangelesmuseumoflove.com) is a fun attraction just by the LA City College. It is not a museum as much as it is a collection of fun activities for couples. My girlfriend and went in the middle of the month on a Staurday. We really enjoyed it. It is full of often silly, but well-thought-out activities ranging from drawing a picture together to being hooked up to a heart rate monitor and starring into each others' eyes till your heart beats match.

## Trip to Arizona

It has been a long time since I left my home state of California, but one of my best friends moved out to Arizona a while back and invited another of our best friends and I to his place for the Thanksgiving weekend. The mad man drove all the way out to LA to pick us up on the 27th and dropped us back off in LA on the 30th. For those of you who don't know how big the United States is, it was a 6 hour drive there and about 8 back (due to holiday traffic). The states might be right next to each other, but it is a vast distance. We had a packed weekend though and our Arizonan friend really wanted to show us around.

On Friday we went to [Chiricahua National Monument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiricahua_National_Monument), a beautiful national park about a 3 hour drive from my friend's home where we were staying (yes, even the state of AZ is vast). We went on one of the shorter trails, but ended up going a little further than we were supposed to (we were in there for at least an hour longer than we planned), throwing off our schedule for the rest of the day. The rock formations are amazing, not seen anything quite like it anywhere else. It is also extremely remote. If you can stomach a long drive it is worth seeing in person.

After Chiricahua we went to the town of [Tombstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tombstone%2C_Arizona). It was already dark by the time we arrived and took about an hour to get there. It is a lovely town with a large and well-preserved western strip, making it a perfect tourist trap. We sadly did not see the worlds larges rose bush, but we did get some amazing fudge before we decided to take the long track back to Phoenix.

Saturday was a stay close day. We went to [Tortilla Flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tortilla_Flat%2C_Arizona) and took a leisurely drive through the [Superstition mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstition_Mountains). After that we hungout around my friend's home for a bit and then saw something that has been on my bucket list for a long while, a Wurlitzer Theater Organ. Turns out there is a pizza place named [Organ Stop Pizza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organ_Stop_Pizza) and they just so happen to have the largest Wurlitzer. The pizza was just okay, but the fact that there is a giant, building sized, organ at a random pizza place in Phoenix is trully wild and not something I expected. 

Sunday was the day to get back to LA. We stopped by the [Riparian Preserve at Water Ranch](https://www.gilbertaz.gov/departments/parks-and-recreation/riparian-preserve-at-water-ranch) early in the morning. It is a large artificial wetland using reclaimed water. There were a lot of birds hanging around so I ended up taking a lot of photos. After that it was about 8 hours of driving with a couple stops along the way to get gas or stretch our legs. A lot of traffic going from AZ to CA and that means car crashes and general traffic. Nevertheless, I got some fun photos of the sunset. 

This trip was also the first time I had food from Waffle House, not amazing food and overpriced, but it is passably better than the majority of other diner chains. We had to take my friend who never been to a diner to another one that was a lot better so he could experience a core aspect of 'american' food culture properly. 

After all that adventure I'm going to take December to decompress.

### News

Some US states [are expanding their censorship laws by banning VPNs](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/11/lawmakers-want-ban-vpns-and-they-have-no-idea-what-theyre-doing). While public VPNs are not a tool for true privacy like they claim, they are tools that can help aid in being private online, get around censorship, protect lives, help students go to school, and employees work remotely. This paired with age verification laws are terrible steps towards making the internet a less safe place. Keeping children safe doesn't mean we need to compromise freedom of speech, risk people's lives (journalists need VPNs), and encourage people to use less safe methods to get around censorship. If there was a true desire to help kids they would invest in education and fight things that actually cause harm. Anyone who argues in favor of age verification and VPN bans is at best maliciously ignorant of what they are doing and it is far more likely that they are aware of the damage they are doing. 

### Cool Projects

The [Pinouts Book](https://pinouts.org) by NODE and Baptiste is an amazing collection of pinout references for many components that electronics tinkerers and makers uses. It is high quality, includes links to official documentation, and was designed to be printable on home printers. NODE also has an [announcement video on their YouTube channel](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EI5PkqR0sjE). 

NODE is on a roll this month, they updated their [Build List](https://buildlist.org) website. This is a website that contains links to manufacturers offering everything from 3D Printing to Book Printing. It lists what services they offer, minimum order amounts, and setup costs for each manufacturer. It is sorted by service, region, and country so you can easily find the closest fab to your location. While it is nice to be able to do things like 3D printing at home, I don't know any people who have a printing press or the equipment to make multilayered PCBs at home. This list will hopefully help you in your future projects and is just another great example of the manufacturing revolution.

Jim Nielsen posted an observation about [AI vs Human mental models](https://blog.jim-nielsen.com/2025/more-context-bro/). He mentions how prompting AI often involves adding more information to a prompt/series of prompts to hopefully nudge the AI into guessing the correct solution for your problem. When humans try to create things efficiently, we often take subtractive approach rather than an additive one, sometimes this leads us to eliminating the problem we wanted to solve in the first place. This kind of thinking expands outside just AI. He mentions NPM dependencies, but I think all human effort focused on quick fixes and quick money fails into this trap. Look at big tech, it is all focused on expanding in some way (increase your productivity, grow the platform, sell more, etc.) rather than sustainable and simple solutions to problems. This kind of thinking creates more complex products while also abstracting any way for us to understand what is happening under the hood. I'm a maximalist and love having every tool for every job, but even I know better than to carry the kitchen sink with me. The capitalist brained tech like generative AI can't ever not be like this because it was built by people who think endless abstraction and growth are the answer. His other post about [AI being pushed into browsers](https://blog.jim-nielsen.com/2025/rip-browsers/), stripping away intentionality and the 'browsing' aspect of the internet kinda reflects my point; AI bros don't understand human thinking, creativity, or really much of anything. 

Speaking of AI browser, Cory Dransfeldt reminds us that they are [inherently headed towards enshittification and may already be harming web accessibility by misusing web standards](https://www.coryd.dev/posts/2025/ai-browsers-are-straight-out-of-the-enshittification-playbook). 

Veronica Explains [how/why she doesn't pay for streaming services anymore](https://vkc.sh/responding-to-comments-on-i-stream-nothing/) (Link to an article that links to her YouTube video) as well as [how she uses Handbrake to transcode her media](https://vkc.sh/handbrake-2025/). With how the world is going, I agree with her on this. Physical media and DRM free digital media are the way to go. It is often cheaper (physical media that is), you actually own it as much as is possible in the modern world, and it gives you a lot of control. A lot of my media is ripped from DVDs and Bluerays, bought from DRM free websites (GOG), or has had the DRM broken. While I have a complex view of piracy, I do download media that is impossible to own (out of print, fan translated, only available via abusive DRM/streaming sites). My switch console is physical only save a few local multiplayer games (I've started backing up those games to my server). A major part of having everything saved to my own systems or being physical is that I have complete control over my consumption. No algorithm is telling me what to watch (thankfully I have friends and good old internet searches to tell me) and my bookshelf is a constant reminder of how many unplayed games and unread books I have so it is less tempting to overconsume. Maybe I should write my own article about this, but Veronica's video and articles really give a good overview of why and how to do it.

Scott shared a short post about [how crazy it is that we have so many inputs due to social media](https://rscottjones.com/the-folly-of-social-media-feeds/) that I want to piggyback on. It truly can be crazy even without social media. Fortunately there are tools like RSS that help a lot. You can filter your inputs and have control without an algorithm choosing for you and you can pick what you want to read/focus on at a given time. You even have the all mighty "Mark All as Read" button to clear out your feeds when they become too great. Our brains were not built for social media and social media was built to exploit that fact. It makes us miserable, builds echo chambers, and creates an environment that is constantly switching context. This goes back to Veronica's reasons for not using streaming, a major one being control over inputs. You will be happier for it.

### Videos

Fraser Builds has [started a series on the history of Alchemy and its transformation into modern chemistry](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7XRxGCqpmVc). He is recreating experiments from primary texts and gives a lot of historical context. I've mentioned his channel in the past, but if you haven't already and are interested in the history of natural philosophy, alchemy, or anything related you must give his videos a watch. Also he is the only one on youtube who seems to have built an alchemy lab from an academic standpoint, that should be worth a watch alone. 

Attoparsec, the guy who made that 1000 key keyboard I shared March, [turned a Vtech Talking Scholar toy into a cyberdeck](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XewX68F0JDg). It even uses the punch cards from the toy to load up programs. It is really cool seeing what people can do with old tech that was destined for landfills otherwise. Raspberry Pi, Arduino, and the other tech that has spawned thanks to their accessibility has not only helped create a maker revolution, but has breathed new life into so much ewaste. It is amazing. The project is really cool and makes me want a cyberdeck myself.

Mitxela made a [fluid simulation pendant](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jis1MC5Tm8k). A little LED matrix display powered by a microcontroller running a FLIP fluid simulation housed in a gold-plated pendant. What an awesome multidisciplinary project. Judging by the viewer count on this video, most people who are interested in this have likely seen it, but this was too cool to not share. He has a [whole write-up on his website](https://mitxela.com/projects/fluid-pendant) as well. 

Polymatt [restored a Chaplet Halikan LA-30A Laptop](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BilLgXkR_Kw). The 5439 Workshop [built a coffee table with a model solar system in it](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=x3iUpv1Wclw). The solar system has planets all following their own orbit and a sun that explodes and then contracts after a period of time. People keep making exceptionally cool coffee tables.

Benn Jordan is back with another great video. This time about those [invasive Flock cameras that popped up everywhere in the US and what we can do about them](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uB0gr7Fh6lY). The cameras are insecure android devices running old versions of android, leak data that can be captured with the right radio equipment, and the marketing behind them is full of outright lies. Perseus Grim talks about [the medieval stasis often found in fantasy stories](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lq7U2CIO81E&t=532s). It is common for fantasy stories to be set in a vaguely European medieval setting, something that people love to complain about. Perseus Grim gives an argument from both an in story reason and reason why the author would write the story set in a setting like this. He acknowledges that most fantasy settings should move forward technologically, but in high magic worlds, worlds with active gods, etc. there might be a good reason society gets stuck in this era of development. There is a lot of good stuff here. Highly recommend watching this video.
